Evidence-based tips for effective feedback

Clinical audit with feedback can be an effective way of improving the quality of healthcare. A Cochrane review of 140 randomised trials found that audit and feedback improved adherence to recommended processes of care by over 4% [1]. That might sound like a modest effect but can be important when it is applied across whole healthcare systems or patient populations. However, the review also found that effects varied a lot between different trials. One quarter of audit and feedback interventions had a relatively large, positive effect on quality of care, while another quarter had a negative or no effect. Larger effects were present when:

  • Baseline performance is low
  • Feedback is delivered via a supervisor or colleague
  • Feedback is provided more than once
  • Feedback is delivered in both verbal and written formats
  • Feedback includes both explicit targets and an action plan.

A more recent paper drew on expert interviews and evidence synthesis to derive 15 state-of-the-science recommendations for effective feedback [2]. These recommendations concern:

  • The nature of the desired action, e.g. improving the specificity of recommendations for action;
  • The nature of the data available for feedback, e.g. providing more rapid or multiple feedback;
  • Feedback display, e.g. minimising unnecessary cognitive workload for recipients;
  • Delivery of feedback, e.g. addressing credibility of information.
